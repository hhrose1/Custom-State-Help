Lease Agreement Red Flags

Important: This page gives general examples of lease red flags. What is legal or enforceable can depend on your state, city, and the exact wording in your lease. This is not legal advice. If something on your lease worries you, talk to a tenant legal aid organization or a licensed attorney in your area.

These are common issues to watch for before you sign, renew, or move in. You can use this as a checklist when reading your lease.

Maintenance & Repairs

Vague or missing maintenance responsibilities

If the lease doesn’t clearly say who handles repairs, appliances, pest control, and HVAC, the landlord may later claim everything is “tenant responsibility.” A good lease says who handles what and how to request repairs.

Fees, Deposits & Money

Excessive or unclear fees

Watch for vague charges like “administrative fees,” “convenience fees,” or unspecified move-in/move-out fees. If a fee is not itemized or explained in writing, that’s a red flag.

No grace period for rent

If late fees start the morning rent is due with no grace period, expect strict enforcement. Some areas require a short grace period by law.

Extremely high or daily late fees

Very high flat late fees or fees that grow daily can add up quickly. Some states cap late fees or require that they be “reasonable.” Anything that seems punitive is a warning sign.

Unclear security deposit rules

A lease should say how much the deposit is, where it is held (if your state requires this), what deductions are allowed, and when it must be returned. Vague or missing deposit rules often lead to disputes at move-out.

Lease Length, Renewal, and Leaving Early

Unfair or missing early termination rules

If breaking the lease means paying all remaining rent with no exceptions, that is a big financial risk. Some leases allow a fixed lease-break fee or re-rental process instead. Look for what happens if you need to move for safety, health, or job reasons.

Automatic renewal without clear notice

Some leases automatically renew for another full term if you don’t give written notice (for example, 60 days before the end date). If you miss the window, you may be locked in for another year. Make sure renewal rules and notice deadlines are clear.

Privacy, Inspections & Landlord Entry

Landlord can enter “at any time for any reason”

Most places require landlords to give notice before entering your home except in an emergency. A lease that allows entry “at any time” with no notice or reason is a serious privacy red flag.

Very restrictive guest rules

Reasonable guest limits are common, but rules that completely ban guests or severely limit overnight visitors can be a sign of a controlling landlord or management company.

Legal Rights, Fair Housing & Discrimination

Clauses that “waive” your legal rights

Be cautious of any statement like “tenant waives right to sue,” “tenant waives right to a jury trial,” or “landlord not responsible for anything, even if negligent.” Many of these are not enforceable, but including them is a major red flag.

Illegal or discriminatory terms

Some examples include blanket bans on emotional support animals where the law requires accommodation, charging extra rent for ESAs, or rules that appear to target certain groups of people. Fair housing protections are different by state, but anything that looks discriminatory deserves a closer look and legal advice.

Hidden rules in “house rules” or extra documents

Leases sometimes push restrictive rules into separate documents like “community policies” or “house rules” that can change at any time. Always ask for copies of every addendum, rulebook, or policy mentioned in the lease before you sign.

Utilities, Cleaning & Extra Requirements

Mandatory move-out cleaning with one specific company

Some leases require you to pay a particular cleaning company at move-out. This can be used to charge inflated or unnecessary fees. It’s a red flag if you have no choice or if prices are not clearly disclosed.

Renter’s insurance required through one provider

Landlords can usually require renter’s insurance, but you should be allowed to choose the insurance company. Being forced to buy through one specific provider can be a sign of unnecessary extra cost.

Utility billing that hides how charges are calculated

Examples include “ratio billing” where utilities are split among tenants but the math is not explained, or flat “utility fees” that can be increased later at the landlord’s discretion. If you can’t tell how your bill is calculated, ask questions before signing.
