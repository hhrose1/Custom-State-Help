Frequently Asked Tenant & Lease Questions

Important: Housing laws and tenant rights are state-specific, city-specific, and lease-specific. This FAQ provides general information only and is not legal advice. Your rights may be different based on where you live and what you signed. For advice about your situation, use the Get Legal Help page to find local tenant legal services.

Before You Sign a Lease

Before signing, carefully review:

  • Rent amount, due date, late fees, and how rent must be paid
  • Which utilities are included versus what you must set up and pay for
  • Security deposit amount, conditions for refunds, and cleaning/damage rules
  • Maintenance and repair responsibilities (who fixes what, and how fast)
  • Guest, pet, parking, storage, and noise rules
  • Move-in / move-out inspection procedures
  • Rules around subletting, re-renting, or assigning your lease
  • Early termination / lease-break policies and any fees

Ask specifically what happens if you need to leave early (job relocation, safety concerns, domestic violence, military orders, etc.). Always ask for changes or promises in writing before you sign.

After You Move In

Always request repairs in writing (email is best). Include:

  • A clear description of the problem
  • Photos or videos if possible
  • When you first noticed the issue
  • Any impact on your health or safety

Keep copies of all messages and responses. If your landlord ignores serious issues, contact tenant legal aid or use the Get Legal Help page.

Rent, Fees & Deposits

Rent escrow is a legal process where, instead of paying rent directly to your landlord, you pay it to a court or government-approved account while major health and safety issues remain unresolved.

Important: Laws about rent escrow are extremely state-specific. In some places, you may be allowed to use rent escrow when:

  • The unit is uninhabitable or unsafe
  • You have given proper written notice about repairs
  • The landlord has been given a reasonable time but has not fixed the problem

Do not stop paying rent or use rent escrow without legal guidance. Improper withholding can lead to eviction. Contact your local tenant legal aid or use the Get Legal Help page for support.

Roommates, Guests & Neighbors

If all roommates are on the same lease, the landlord may treat all tenants as jointly responsible for the full rent. If one roommate leaves, the remaining tenants may still owe the full amount.

To protect yourself:

  • Use a written roommate agreement that explains how rent and utilities are split
  • Keep track of who pays what and when
  • Communicate early if someone is considering leaving

If you’re unsure of your obligations, review your lease and seek legal guidance if needed.

  • Automatic renewal: lease continues unless you give written notice by a certain date

Always ask:

  • “How much notice do I need to give if I don’t want to renew?”
  • “What rent increase should I expect at renewal?”

Evictions & Getting Legal Help

Eviction is a legal process where a landlord asks a court for permission to remove a tenant from a rental unit. Common reasons include nonpayment of rent, serious lease violations, or illegal activity.

Basic protections (which vary by state) often include:

  • Written notice before an eviction case is filed
  • The right to a court hearing
  • The right to present evidence and defenses
  • Landlords generally cannot lock you out, shut off utilities, or remove your belongings without a court order

Evictions can affect your credit and rental history, so it is important to respond to notices and court dates immediately.

You may be able to get help from:

  • Local tenant legal aid organizations
  • University or law school legal clinics (if you are a student)
  • Tenant unions or housing advocacy groups
  • State or city housing agencies

Use the Get Legal Help page on this site to find resources that are specific to your state or area.

General Best Practices for Tenants

  • Keep all important communication in writing (email is best)
  • Photograph your unit at move-in, during issues, and at move-out
  • Read your lease fully and ask questions before signing
  • Report problems early, especially anything involving safety, water, or pests
  • Seek legal or advocacy support early if you feel pressured or unsure
